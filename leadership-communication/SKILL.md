# Leadership Communication Skill

Draft and review communications from the Head of Data's Office to leadership and the -1 layer.

## The Core Question

Every leadership communication comes down to one question:

**Does this land the strategic intent in a way that earns the audience's collaboration?**

Not "is it comprehensive?" Not "is it detailed enough?" — does the audience walk away knowing what's changed, why it matters, and what's expected of them?

## Before Drafting

Gather context first:

1. **What is the strategic intent?** What decision has been made, or what direction is being set? State it in one sentence.
2. **What is the audience?** Leadership forum, -1 layer, cross-functional peers, CXO? Each requires different altitude.
3. **What is the format?** Presentation (visual aid + verbal), read-ahead (standalone), or talking points (speaker reference)?
4. **Is this an announcement or a proposal?** Announcements state decisions. Proposals invite input. The tone is fundamentally different — never confuse the two.
5. **What is the ask?** Every communication must have one. Compliance, collaboration, championship, sign-off — name it before drafting.
6. **Who has already aligned?** CXO sign-off, lead alignment, peer buy-in — state the alignment chain. It signals to the room that this has been pressure-tested.

## Communication Principles

### 1. Direction over process

Lead with strategic intent, not operational machinery. "We chose to run lean — here's what that means" beats "Here's the framework for goal planning."

Process is a downstream consequence. If the direction is clear, the process conversation writes itself. Detailed frameworks feel like a hammer to leaders who value autonomy of thought.

**Test:** If you removed all process detail, would the audience still understand the direction and their role? If yes, cut the process.

### 2. Principles before prescription

State 2-3 beliefs first. They earn permission to prescribe. Ground rules come after the collaborative ask, never before it.

**The arc that works:**
1. Deliberate choice (what we decided and why)
2. Conditions (what needs to be true for this to work)
3. Substance with examples (what this looks like in practice)
4. Collaborative ask (what we need from you)
5. Ground rules (what's firm)
6. Aspiration (where we're headed)
7. Acknowledgment (who helped shape this)

**Test:** Does the audience encounter the "why" before the "what"? Do they see the principles before the rules?

### 3. Concrete vocabulary over abstract tiers

Leaders need language they can use in their own planning conversations. Abstract categories ("Dedicated tier," "Contributed tier") don't stick. Concrete work types ("RCA," "Data Products," "Experiment guardrails") do.

**Test:** After the presentation, can a leader say "this goal needs analyst-led support on RCA" in their own planning meeting? If the vocabulary doesn't transfer, it's too abstract.

### 4. Two-sided framing for cross-functional audiences

Always answer both: what Analytics brings AND what the business team brings. One-sided framing ("here's what we do") creates turf reactions.

"The goal is always yours" as an opening line defuses autonomy concerns. Frame engagement modes from the leader's perspective — what they get and what's expected of them.

**Test:** For each engagement mode or category, can the audience see their own role? If it only describes what Analytics does, reframe.

### 5. Collaborative tone, firmness in sequencing

Invitation first, firmness second. Both are present — sequencing matters.

- "Engage with your AM" not "Book by March 20 or default to Self-Serve"
- Timelines belong in ground rules, not in the collaborative ask
- Ground rules are non-negotiable but positioned after the invitation, not before it

**Test:** Does the ask feel like a partnership or a compliance exercise? Would a leader feel respected or managed?

### 6. Self-serve as aspiration, not demotion

Position as "org stepping up" not "Analytics stepping back." Make self-serve feel substantial with real examples — not a dumping ground.

The championship ask to leadership is critical: adoption is a leadership behaviour, not just an analytics initiative.

**Test:** Would a leader feel good about their team being in the self-serve category? If it feels like a consolation tier, reframe.

## Content Architecture

### Section design

Each section should pass three tests:
1. **Standalone scannable** — a leader who only reads this section gets the point
2. **Verbally expandable** — the presenter can add 60-90 seconds of context without the slide changing
3. **One idea per section** — if it has two ideas, split it

### What goes on the page vs. what the presenter says

The page is a visual aid, not a document. The presenter provides the narrative.

| On the page | In the presenter's mouth |
|---|---|
| Lean bullets, short phrases | Full sentences, context, motivation |
| Work type names with one-line descriptions | Stories about why each work type matters |
| The ask in 1-2 lines | The reasoning behind the ask |
| Ground rules as numbered callouts | The "why this is non-negotiable" |

### Visual format (Notion presentation pages)

- **Callouts** for emphasis, action items, and ground rules. Color-code by function: blue for asks, yellow for rules, green for aspiration, red for problems.
- **Two-column layouts** for side-by-side comparisons (engagement modes, what we do vs. what you do, problem vs. solution).
- **Tables** only when comparing structured data across rows. Not for prose.
- **Bullets over prose.** Short phrases, not sentences.
- **Speaker notes in toggle** — full talking points with timing cues for the presenter. Always include these.
- **Opening context line** — duration, approval status, alignment chain. Sets expectations.

### Italic descriptors under categories

When presenting a taxonomy (e.g., engagement modes, work types), the italic line under each category name is the most important line. It must:
1. Be instantly parseable — no jargon that requires a follow-up question
2. Describe the engagement pattern from the audience's perspective
3. Differentiate clearly from other categories

**Good:** *org can take forward given framework, guardrails, validation*
**Bad:** *driving functional maturity*

**Test:** If a leader reads only the category name + italic, do they know what it means for them?

## Audience Calibration

### Leadership forum (CXO + VPs)

- Altitude: strategic direction, not operational detail
- Time: 5-10 minutes max
- Ask: collaboration, compliance, championship
- Tone: confident (decision is made), inviting (their input shaped it)
- Detail level: principles + examples + ask. No rubrics, no scoring mechanisms.
- Always acknowledge CXO/leadership role in shaping the direction

### -1 layer (Senior leads, AMs, STLs)

- Altitude: operational translation of the direction
- Time: 30-60 minutes, interactive
- Ask: execute within the framework, provide feedback, flag failure modes
- Tone: direct, collaborative, open to challenge on implementation
- Detail level: rubrics, mechanisms, worked examples, edge cases
- This audience can handle (and wants) the operational machinery

### Cross-functional peers

- Altitude: "what does this mean for our teams working together?"
- Time: varies
- Ask: specific coordination or alignment
- Tone: peer-to-peer, no hierarchy signaling
- Detail level: only what affects joint work

## Iteration Process

### The pattern that works

1. **Build detailed** — start with the full framework, all the detail
2. **Test with CXO** — get the strategic nudge (usually: simplify, lead with principles)
3. **Simplify to principles** — strip to 2-3 beliefs + concrete examples
4. **Test with leads** — get operational feedback (naming collisions, edge cases)
5. **Adapt for org context** — rename terms that collide with existing frameworks
6. **Strip for presentation** — lean guiding points, let the presenter expand
7. **Iterate on framing** — specific examples from the org, not generic analyst-team language
8. **Land** — present, then follow up with operational detail through AMs/-1 layer

**Key insight:** Each audience layer compresses the message. What survives all rounds of compression is the actual message. If something keeps getting cut across iterations, it wasn't essential.

### Feedback incorporation

- When leadership pushes back on meticulousness: lead with principles, put detail in appendix or follow-up
- When leaders push back on autonomy: reframe to show their role and agency
- When naming collides with existing org frameworks (e.g., DARCI): rename without hesitation — the concept matters, the label doesn't
- When the framing feels like "process": anchor on team direction, let process be a downstream consequence
- When the problem statement feels generic: use the org's actual problems, not analyst-team archetypes

## Anti-Patterns

| Anti-pattern | Why it fails | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Leading with the framework | Feels like a hammer. Leaders disengage before hearing the "why" | Lead with direction and beliefs |
| One-sided framing | "Here's what Analytics does" triggers turf reactions | Show both sides: what we bring + what you bring |
| Abstract tiers without examples | Labels don't stick without concrete work types | Name the actual work: RCA, Data Products, Experiments |
| Compliance tone in the ask | "Book by X or default to Y" feels punitive | Collaborative invitation first, timelines in ground rules |
| Generic problem statements | "Analytics teams struggle with..." doesn't resonate | Use the org's actual problems: "goal planning is delayed because..." |
| Self-serve as leftover | Signals that these teams aren't important enough | Make self-serve substantial with real examples, position as aspiration |
| Process before principles | Rubrics and mechanisms before the audience understands why | Principles earn permission to prescribe |
| Skipping CXO acknowledgment | Misses the chance to signal co-creation | "Thank you for the iterations that shaped this" |

## Relationship to Other Skills

- **document-quality-review:** Evaluates structure, flow, vocabulary, writing clarity. Use as a follow-up check on the finished communication.
- **proposal-review:** Evaluates whether a proposed solution solves a business problem. Use when the communication IS a proposal, not an announcement.
- **mbr-review:** Evaluates operational honesty in status documents. Different domain entirely.
- **leadership-communication (this skill):** Evaluates strategic framing, audience calibration, and whether the communication lands the intent. Use when the challenge is "how do I present this?" not "is the document well-written?"

These skills can be combined. A leadership communication that's well-framed but poorly structured might benefit from document-quality-review as a polish pass.
